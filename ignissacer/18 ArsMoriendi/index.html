Since he disagreed with his brother’s belief, he deemed that a justification for homicide. The dialectic of belief or no belief is itself as simple-minded, when one is too ignorant to understand the essence of belief, which is wavering and fragile, subject to sudden upheaval and deletion, nothing as substantial as life. People too easily abstract enemy beliefs onto the faces of people and kill those faces as a means to killing those beliefs. Human life comes to mean less than orthodoxy.

Dante’s escape was quite easy but unsurprising considering his captors were themselves criminals. The smuggler society had once stretched through thousands of tunnels vibrant with the pace of a dangerous life, but now only a weak few survived the legacy. Many nostalgic, anticipated a quixotic return to the old days—the grand price of the painting their expected savior. However, not all were too foolish in their dedication to let an enticing offer pass them by. The guard, a father in his late 50’s, had seen the glory days of the smugglers but also many knives at his throat and buried in the backs of his friends. Like any noble parent, he wished the best for his offspring.

Not all smugglers recognized Dante as the son of Dr. Allemanne. In his face though, the guard saw the face of Dr. Allemanne, who he knew as a wealthy man, a face that often appeared in tandem with the face of diplomats, politicians, CEOs — the important people that other dominant cultures followed. The story of Dr. Allemanne’s materialistic demise had not trickled through the tavern conversations that comprised the smugglers information network. The guard believed he could extort quite a sum from Dante in return for Dante’s escape. Since the smugglers had long been in decline, they had been living off what they could hunt or the little their slow wrinkled hands could steal. What kind of a sum should be demanded? The guard did not know.

Dante handed him half of his father’s trust.

Before his trip, he surprised Ms. Steinhurst disguised as a criminal and forced her to take out all of Dante’s trust. Believing a criminal had actually stolen the money, the bank’s insurance replaced it and Dante increased his fund by a fraudulent 50%.

He lost that 50% to the guard. The guard provided directions to the forest and a map for his escape. He even gave Dante some of his clothes so he could blend in better. The guard would take the same trail. Even at the height of its decline, the smuggler’s code was still enforced with expected brutality. He could not be caught. The auction would soon begin.

Dante ignored the guard’s recommendation. He wanted to find Gabriel, but feared he would be recognized under the scrutiny of a questionable request of release. Instead he made his way to the auction house. He had his full trust in coin to purchase the painting—the gateway to a pleasant second life with his father. The painting held so much hope in it for the smugglers, for the brothers, for the many wealthy citizens of the world now arriving at the most infamous auction house in the world.

House Dekales in Tyffyn was world renown. If one wanted to find the most rare or exotic of goods, House Dekales is the only place to go. Sure one must ignore the illegal manner in which the goods found themselves at the House, but for those of the type that could afford to attend these auctions, pushing out such thoughts was a daily task.

House Dekales was nicknamed the “House of Extortion” sounding at first listen to “House of Execution,” a planned misconstrual by the House’s adversaries. The smugglers stole a 17th c. Monet or roman statue and the only way to have it requited was to win it at an auction. Police could not be involved for it would break the unwritten code and surely disrupt the marvelous marketplace. The wealthy considered the occasional extortion then a mere fee for their entry to this elite and exclusive market.

A cobbled road wound up the craggy knoll from which the cantilevered House Dekales perched. Road maintenance had been long disregarded, so dodging a piece of wall or withstanding a brutal pothole was expected. At the top of the hill, before the entrance of the House, parked the coaches of the wealthy. A vista emerged of the incredible density of the Pyfn forest, which disappeared into blackness before meeting the horizon.

The House had been carved into the slouching mountainside, the entrance many stories below the peak of a bell tower. From the front, the House looked to be only a couple stories tall but in fact raised four times that. The smugglers did not build the house; it was an artifact of royal wealth millennia ago when man was audacious enough to spend a generation turning an inhospitable boulder into a magnificent home.

The entryway was grand and retained its medieval technology. Two giant wooden doors splayed out in front of a half-oval doorway that could accommodate twenty men marching through side-by-side. Dangled just inside, above the entryway and lying in wait, was a barbed iron gate meant by design to keep undesirables out, but principally used by the smugglers to keep the desirables in, if anything suspicious occurred.

Gabriel dulled his knife with the constant push and pull through the peat walls of the hut. Now he looked for Gamut.

Dante approached House Dekales,

“The auction will be begin at. . .1 geld! Now do I have a bid of 1 geld?”

The auction began with some cheaper items only meant to draw out the suspense. Everyone wanted the painting. No one wanted a decorative pot or drinking mug. Today, they had traveled the many miles and risked their dutiful upstanding lives for only the most prized item that would be the blather of their friends and covetous acquaintances, the painting Christ Carrying the Cross.

Gabriel reached the base of the hill holding up House Dekales. From this perspective, he saw how the road of disrepair curved around the hill and then split into many arterial roads that could take one to any of the seven seas in the world. Gabriel believed Gamut would hide his chariot somewhere along this road for an easy escape, likely behind the house where there wouldn’t be any traffic or eyes peering from above.

Dante’s heart beat fast. He was sure he would be caught. There were too many of his father’s friends in the crowd. He pulled the hood tighter, but the heat of the congested room was unbearable. Sweat flowed steadily down the contours of his face. He wiped it again and again in rhythmic fashion.

A vase sold for 10 geld. It was the second to last item.

Gabriel made his way along the road, and saw in front of him Gamut standing behind his chariot examining the painting with Drummond. Though difficult to see from his vantage point, Drummond appeared not as a man bartering freedom for his family but as a man grieving over their tragic fate. Gabriel crept closer, positioning himself behind some stray rocks behind the two men. His bright blue eyes against the pale glow of his skin caught Drummond’s eye. Drummond pretended to not notice him, but as he accepted the large pouch of coin, he gestured towards the front of the chariot. Gamut nodded and they walked towards the stallions with their backs to Gabriel. Gabriel’s chance had come. He could sneak into the back of the wagon.

After much ceremonial pomp and circumstance, the painting was unveiled and bidding began at the unheard of 100-geld price. The first to bid was an exotic dilettante prince of some poor dictatorship.

“101 Geld, “called out a middle-aged matron behind Dante.

“105 Geld,” echoed in the corner.

“110 Geld,” snapped at the previous bid’s heel.

And so it began. The smugglers hope, Dante’s hope, the hope of rich and famous hung on this event.

Gabriel crept into the back of the chariot. He could hear Drummond outside telling an anecdote about a horse he had as a boy. As the conversation drew to a natural close, he rapped twice feigning a fidgety demeanor. A warning, Gabriel thought. Gabriel stowed himself behind some luggage. His finger throbbed in disproportional pain.

“201 Geld”

“211 Geld, new bidder!”

“250 Geld”

“300 Geld”

“375 Geld”

Dante held out.

"A warning, 375 million," announced the auctioneer.

The bidding was methodical among the anxious palpable crowd.

Every new bid followed by collective exasperation.

“300 Geld, 400, 425, 450, 475, 490, 525!” The impatience pushed the bidding up, faster.

“550 Geld!”

“600 Geld!”

“650!”

“750”

In seconds, the cost doubled, then tripled, burgeoning to a bid of 1500 Geld.

The bid surprised everyone, surprised Dante though it came from his mouth.

“This is to be a record, my fare men and women. Fair warning, I am selling it this time. Going once, going twice,-”

A commotion erupted at the back of the hall. An old smuggler with a torch barrelled through as fast as the crowd could part.

“Its a fake. I’m sorry I sold the real one to a man from Anderseite. I’m a terrible traitor and come now to admit my grave mistake, please do, as you must. Oh, I am such a despicable traitor!” Drummond thrust himself forward, opened his grip and hurled the torch at the painting.

Drummond was struck down with a successive shots from the crowd.

Smugglers returned shots at his attacker, a distinguished man brandishing an antique pistol, far too slow to reload.

All manner of defensive arms emerged as the scene collapsed into chaos of fleeing, death, blood, and visceral action.

Dante abandoned the suffocating robe and ran around two men exchanging death by sword.

Gabriel had been on the road for two weeks stowed in the back the coach, and unable to carry out his plan of stealing the chariot. His hand throbbed beneath the makeshift wrapping on his hand. A disgusting odor and sign of blackened gangrene skin infested Gabriel’s nose. He gagged at the sight. His cut was infected. The gangrene ebbed down his arm, from the knick on his finger and invaded his blood. His body succumbed under the stress of a fever, weakened further by the weeks of a poor diet. Gabriel lay in a damp pool of sweat subject to an early death and evident of his existence borne into a trap. Alessandria wondered eternal.

Dr. Samekh, now after his vows known as Brother Vehere, kneeled down for his evening prayer. Throughout the day, at each prayer he noticed a gathering in the yard below around Dr. Allemanne, the tree. The crowd grew with the forward arch of the sun and beyond the suns disappearance. Now the tree was alight in the gleam of a hundred angered torches.

Rumors and gossip were sins, and conversation consisted rarely of anything but interpretations of the Bible, so all the news Dr. Allemanne heard came from what he overheard patrons discussing after the end of the service. That evening before his final prayer, he heard of a gathering at Yggdrasil by Peir and his followers.

Gathered around the tree were insular minds of the average populace, contributing nothing to culture and on a pathway of returning to the earth having made no change upon the world. An intellectually inferior man of action, Peir Gerlofs led the group. Considering his obvious lack of appreciation for education, he strived to make cogent arguments about the path of society, but always equated this path with that which birthed a genocidal maniac and his both active and passive followers, all of whom were accessories to a murder of millions. Peir Gerlofs and his followers, like those of the manic, stared with the dead eyes of an instinctual animal, unable to actively process the true meaning of their actions.

Peir Gerlofs had long preached claims of vivid images of death and suffering that came from the supposed black magic in the tree. Subsequently the media reprinted these images encouraging them to be repeated frequently in conversation, heightening the emotional arousal in the sensitive average; they became victims of their own subconscious acting irrationally and protective. Though the probability and likelihood of black magic very low, the ruminations of potential discomfort were too difficult to ignore, thus logic was ignored.

Martina had founded this group but could not cure their emergent toxic ideology. They were a volatile mass driven by pluralistic ignorance, resistant to all argument and truthful revelation. Martina tried to point out the flaws of their reasoning, but was ostracized. No single member willing to admit that they do not believe what they believed all believed.

Match strikes, torches thrown, and in seconds am I bleeding energy with the efficiency of a healthy flame. The bright orange and reds are reflected in the heavens.

The timid crowd retreats surprised by the fierce wave of splendid energy warming their cold faces.

Yggdrasil transforms into a beacon of light, radiating red-yellow into all corners of town.

Red-yellows color the sky.

Burnt wood fills chill air; smoke rises into the cosmos; cinders fall.

Alessandria waits at In Vino Veritas for Gabriel, whom time will never bring.

Dr. Samekh kneels, eyes-closed, with slow steady breaths at final prayer.

The longest, thickest branch, Yggdrasil’s mast, swings disconnected from the trunk and obliterates the tower.

Dr. Samekh and the church collapse onto Peir’s, onto his torched followers.

An ethereal song fades in. The notes tell the tale of life. The music expresses joy itself, sadness itself, elation itself—emotions stripped from their contextual stories.
