Synopsis

A wealthy but terminally-ill psychologist becomes a hermit due to ergot poisoning in a quasi-European not quite modern, not quite passed time. In his hermit's tree, he is often visited by his Faustian sons, a mosaic of a man, and his mentee, the mayor.

This is the story of the final weeks leading up to his death hinged on the discovery of a cherished painting: The Temptation of St. Anthony by Hieronymus Bosch.

1. Prologue

Smoke of a dense central furnace disintegrating into a diaphanous periphery flowed up in a slow sway of frigid final breath.

With the fall of a hammer I was free. Free of the constant burden of respiration. Free of the irrational fear of death. Free of my last possession, the final artifact of my consumerism: Hieronymus Bosch’s The Temptation of St. Anthony—an exquisite priceless piece that is a surreal evocation of the ever inspirational theme of St. Anthony’s temptations in the desert. Anthony, who resisted temptress phantoms and Demons manifested as spurious offers of wealth and violent tormenters that infiltrated his every waking moment, is appealed to now for deliverance from an infectious disease of the skin named in his direct honor, St Anthony’s Fire.

St. Anthony’s fire is caused by ergot fungus in spoiled rye. Symptoms include: seizures, spasms diarrhea, parenthesis, itching, headaches, nausea, vomiting, and gangrene that emerges at the fingertips and toe tips then spreads until at once the dead, black tissue meets as one, united.

Athanasius of Alexandria put all the stories of St Anthony to paper in an influential biography Vita S Antoni. The spread of St Anthony’s life’s stories and the ideas they withheld led to the rise of monasticism across the west. This is the story of all that precedes the hammer fall. Like consciousness, it is a confluence of thoughts and ideas, of both natural and human artistic expressions, concise but dense with mind-wandering nutrients, which I can only hope precipitates a fraction of Vita S Antoni’s influence.

2. Volatile

"Dante, you devilish boy, unroll that dog this minute!" commanded Ms. Steinhurst.

"You object as if you are his counsel. But it is obvious he is asleep and seeks none!”

"Ah, but if that blanket-wrapped sausage wakes and takes your nose to be a sausage, will you not seek counsel?"

"Fine, fine, only a bit of fun with old Pushkin"

"Old indeed! More respect for your Elders is a point in which you must work on. Where is your brother, Gabriel?"

"Off on his knees babbling on about Mother, no doubt"

"How dare you insult your late Mother!"

"I am sorry, Madam, but he never plays and I am forever bored without another playmate"

"Go, go join him, be in company with him for the day, and then you will not be bored nor so black-tongued."

“But Madam my tongue is pink, or is the well so facetious as to deceive me?”

“The well does not deceive you. The tongue is black in metaphor. The things you say are dark and as your tongue is the principal facilitator of speech, it must also be dark. Now, do as I say and join your brother.”

“Yes, Madam”

I, a distinguished but self-absorbed professor of psychological physics, entered this exchange unprepared, my capable mind burdened with internal ideas uninterested in the external.

“What would you like for dinner doctor?” asked Ms. Steinhurst.

After a long moment’s hesitation, I replied, “No, no dinner, I ate too much bread at lunch.”

I made my way from the foyer to relaxation in my study, cigar alit and wine in hand. In residence was a 19th century globe, an ornate chandelier, generous glass bar, large leather chairs, well-placed antique lamps upon antique end tables, paintings of social import, a mammoth antique Ferghan rug atop the centuries old cherry wood floor—it was quite a demonstration of wealth, of success one might say. I sat in my favorite chair, which was well worn from years of erosion by my backside. I relaxed there and considered my Faustian sons who seemed to be my personal genetic representation of a holy war: one devilish and one angelic. They were yet innocent boys, and in this stage of the story no more evil or angelic than normal. But they were very much on opposite ends of the continuum, bookended by utmost evil and utmost good, left to right or right to left is inconsequent, decide your alignment for the world itself is indecisive. On to musings my mind thrust and the pen followed.

Patterns govern our existence. Patterns of neurons contrive a vile existence of an ever present “us versus them.” Infants, at three months, see race; it is how they are wired, how the neurons are patterned. The infants grow into adults of frightful affiliation to others of strong but also tenuous connection. From an infantile reflex arises this pattern of connections in an equally infantile goal of defending even spurious relations. A mere perception of relatedness allows us to risk our lives in the name of our brothers or motherland, those bastards of relation in name alone, which reason rejects. Call us a fraternity and we shall die for it. That is a reflex. Automatic. Without thought.

Yet I still cry at the cruelty of the beauty of this us versus them world that I am fit only a few decades for. I aim to perceive everyday in a fuller way: like a shattered mirror reflects light in infinite variety. So, too, do I want to perceive life in infinite variety. To this will is my conscious subservient. To this conscious, wills my will a new portrait of life. To this will I venture to shatter my life into pieces, to form that new portrait: a mosaic.

This will be it named I will not name To name it, would be to arrogate that I could hold such a position. Instead a description suffices. The will is my contention of my place in an allegorical cave imprisoned in a society of prisoners who are excluded to mere shadows of existence. I do not have the luxury of free movement like the philosopher, unrestrained by the physical realities of the world. Rather I am imprisoned by the laws of nature, of friction and pressure, of gravity and tension. I am a prisoner not turned towards mere shadows, but towards the source of the shadows. The source is our psychology; the shadows are all that we experience.. The shadows are irrational emotions and insufficient thinking, and the prisoners hold them as complete truth. Knowledge of the external is valued but knowledge of the internal is not. There is no bible for the brain. The collective psychologists and I are writing it.

I contend that I as a psychologist am requisite to explain to the entire myriad malfunctions undesired by a rational mind. Cognitive and motivational biases number in the hundreds but few are trained in their recognition. Few can rein in irrational emotion. Few can withhold the impulse to hurt others both physically and materialistically instead of solving the underlying causes. Man goes through the motions of life without ever understanding the basis of life. Man tries. Man ascribes supernatural origins for cognitive processes and even names these origins under an only humanistic ideal of omniscience. And this naming gives rise to conflict between prisoners, for one name must be better than another name. An insult of a name is an insult of the believer, of what they arranged their lives around, of their intelligence, so an insult must be eliminated lest their existence fall to pieces. I intervene to describe the true source of the shadows but fall victim myself to impulse. I can only educate as much as they listen.

Thus, I seek to transcend burdened ways, and live a life unattached to this world of prisoners. To be free I must relinquish all ties to this world. For they are just that, ties, prison restraints that do not allow me to think with my mind completely open and will never let me leave in the fashion that I must. I must not turn my mind inward and east into a fortress against all that touches skin. I must turn my mind inward and outward and respect the ideas of the future and meditations of the past.

I do not consider the eternal cycle to be the Der Sinn des Lebens rather I consider Der Sinn des Lebens ist sterben. Death defines life and definition is meaning. Death is the one source of all pain. All fear is a derivative of death. All happiness is to laugh at death. All sadness is to remember death. All motivation is to succeed before death. All despair is in the face of death. It reigns as the pinnacle punishment and pinnacle right of immortal honor. Death is not to be sought. If so, all this writing has been fruitless. Rather a life is and must be experienced with death. And this experience is stressful. It is horrible. It is a tearing and clawing experience. It is life. The threat of death grasps the gut. The threat is eternal as I am not. The threat can only be temporarily ignored. So we must commit to this Drange, to a greater feeling of relief from burden, than any neurochemical reaction our genetics could indirectly create as dopamine released, than any religious order or spirituality has achieved. We must acknowledge that the meaning of life is death, and if it’s purpose you seek, then you seek it all your life, and your purpose becomes all that approached death-

I fell into convulsions, fell out of the chair, fell into a coma as my brain ventured to assess the damage, and recover from the irritation of a self-electrocution; my ranting precipitated and precluded by a flight of frontotemporal epilepsy.

At any one moment in time (besides those in seizing of course), our brains are poised on the precipice of entropy, manifest as seizure. The fluidity of the metabolic processes of the brain, and the maintenance of the environment necessary for these processes, are held to such exact standards that a single flux in either metabolism or environment yields an act of chaos. A change of blood pH by just 0.10 sends the body spiraling towards the grave, or an organized effort by multiple neuronal networks—enabled by constant activation of aspartate and glutamate receptors can —without the inhibition of GABA (the brains ever-watchful chemical conscience) — ripple like a wave in a stadium, through the brain and send me spiraling from my chair. The brain does not tend to entropy, as it has evolved to avoid it at all costs, but does not always succeed.

Floors and walls bathed in sterile white. My eyes incurred flashes of pain. Infinitesimal barbed photons streaked at a speed of the function of c through my eyelids into my exposed retinas. The pupils shrunk in slow retreat from the borders of my eyes; the pain ebbed.

Consciousness faded in. It did not turn on like the switch that released the biting light. No, consciousness exists in a continuous state, not a binary one, and in fluid motion slid from oblivion to awareness.

“Dr. Allemanne can you hear me?”

“This is Dr. Rotkod from Revelation Hospital. You suffered a seizure.”

“A seizure, am I brain damaged?” I asked, as I processed complex formulas and concepts in my own self-assessment, “Prime numbers 3, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19. . .Square root of 1,987 is 44.57. . .”

“No, there was no permanent damage. The seizure was quite mild.”

“What could have caused a seizure? I have never had one in my life.”

Satisfied with my self-assessment, I perused memories of childhood, memories of times fallen and times in hospital. No signs of seizure-derived amnesia here. Everything seems well.

“It was caused by Ergotism we believe.”

“And what is Ergotism?”

“It is a poison caused by ingestion of ergot, a rye fungus.”

“I have never heard of that.”

“But you have likely heard of the result of ergotism, if untreated. It is the same disease that originated the idea of witches and subsequent deluded hunts for them in Salem. Hunters glimpsed the supernatural from an anomaly of neurochemistry, but did not see it in a vicious god, rather in certain women.”

“Have you eaten any rye lately?” asked his curious nurse.

In a fit of scatterbrain, I had ingested a piece of bread bought earlier than my mental model of the world had attributed thus acquiring the disease, or so my associates hypothesized. My peers and family in response to the sudden onset of my hermetic behavior adopted ergotism as an appropriate diagnosis. Only a disease associated with hallucinations, convulsions and other affections of the mind could cause me to relieve myself of all possessions and then in that end, also meet my end, as a living, breathing entrapped human, or so at least society dictates. However, it was not a simple infection that led to such a dramatic shift in my life’s work; it could not have been. The ergot plays an influence throughout this story, but at that time of such enlightenment I assure you I was free, and influenced by something greater. I believe it to have been something much greater, something that Anthony was in ally with. With the loss of that piece of art, I was not expressing an indirect wish of the infection, but my farewell to a reliance on objects of affection from which to derive myself through introspection. It was something of a great but so easily translucent power I served in that cavernous tree, until that final possession of mine was found, then sold, thereby allowing me to exasperate into bliss!

Dr. Rotkod’s voice reeled me in from atemporal tangents, "Good news is that you are stable now and will make a full recovery from the ergotism.”

“Terrific news. Am I free to leave? I must get back to my office. I have not had a drink in ages.”

“You were here only a day, Sir. That is hardly a long time to be missing a drink. In fact you will be required to miss a drink for much longer.”

“For how long? On what grounds doctor? A bit of ergot cannot take away a man’s tolerance! I’d say if anything, I’m stronger now for having survived.”

“I am afraid we have some bad news. During our tests we discovered something serious.”

He paused to let me prepare for the worst. The visual cortex gave me a slideshow in seconds of everything my imagination could conceive would come out of his mouth.

“You should never have another single drink. You have a tumor developing in your liver. It appears stable for now, but in the coming weeks it will only become worse. The tumor is inoperable.”

“Inoperable. Surely you could at least try, no?”

“The tumor has assimilated into the liver. To remove it would mean removing the entire liver and at your age a transplant is not feasible.”

“What about treatment?”

“The presumably high alcohol diet has restricted the tumor’s growth somewhat, but any known drugs will also kill the liver. The assimilation is simply too far. We can begin treatment and pain management, but we can only realistically hope for another year.”

“There is nothing else you can do?”

“No, I am afraid we can only provide small doses of very targeted radiation in hopes of killing the tumor cells, but not the healthy liver cells.”

“I read often about stem cells. Couldn’t you regrow any of the dead cells?”

“No the studies are still inconclusive. The treatment has been very inconsistent in rats, and humans add another thick layer of complexity.”

I had distracted myself with questions I knew the answer to. I had difficulty accepting this news, as anyone would. The end always seems a worry of the far future, yet years creep in without the knowledge of that perspective. The perspective becomes unknowingly outdated until confronted by the harshness of life.

“There is another option.”

The doctor’s curious porosity broke my meditation.

“There is an experimental treatment in development here at this hospital that has passed all animal trials and has begun human trials. My colleagues are seeking out volunteers for the procedure. The procedure is called: Selective Memory Eradifiction.TM

“Selective memory eradication? You can erase my memory selectively? Is this a new procedure?”

“A very new procedure invented by my colleagues Dr. Bartle and Dr. Zotak who work in research here at the Hospital. It is an alternative we hope to offer our terminal patients in lieu of typical pain management regiments, or currently illegal assisted suicides. We will erase your knowledge of the terminal disease, including for example the conversation we are having now, and every hour spent here.

“The latter sounds brilliant!” I interject.

“Um…yes… you will leave this office without a single coherent memory of anything that has happened. You will believe to have been subject to an ordinary physical, nothing else.”

“But I will leave still but months from death? And what about the pain? The complications of the disease? Won’t they just return the patients here?”

“Intelligent observation. Erasure of memory of course is not a cure. However there is an extended placebo effect, as we refer to it in the lab. By being unaware of the disease, the symptoms are ignored by the body. Ignorance of the disease lessens psychological stress, which impairs the immune system. It is a phenomenon we still seek an explanation for. For an unknown reason a loss of awareness produces this effect, but the absence does not. A patient pre-diagnosis then will suffer despite not knowing what it is they suffer from, while a patient post diagnosis, who has no more conscious knowledge of his condition does not suffer. Well actually what if it is sub. . .” The doctor lowered his voice to a murmur as he stared off into space lost in his own musings. With a jolt of sudden self-awareness, snapped back to consciousness. “So, your choices are hospice or perhaps a year more of happiness from SMETM."

I chose SMETM. I chose to forget the diagnosis and to live my last few weeks as well as I could. I did this for my sons. I exchanged a longer life potential for the ability to give my sons more memories, better memories, memories of trips, eye-opening conversations and my pride in them. Cancer will not plague even the slightest remembrance of me. Their memory will not suffer due to my suffering. The neurons that can in a miracle of design express father will not be forever entwined with those that express cancer and the inevitable flood of emotions attached to it. Cancer will not be remembered, and the past must be better flavored. I exchanged my memories for their memories. I exchanged their forthcoming life, for their past life. Their past life had been that which in memory only brought melancholy. They had only seen me through the distorted gloom of a brown bottle, and I had only seen them after the bottle. A brain resembles a sponge, but mine had been one, withholding naught but alcohol and contempt.

All things coveted are of earth and of earth only comes pain. I must empty my life of earth, and prepare to live. I must give my sons all the potential memories in the hours of the weeks that followed, and ingrain in them the knowledge of a dead man. I had to forget all that led me to this epiphany, so I did, and so such a fairy tale death was possible. My apprehensive reader, you may doubt my euphemistic description, but in this current relationship I hold all the information, and in time smirking revelation will become you, and you will realize the magic of my untragic last breath.

3. Father

I exited the house onto Vidrar Way, a few (thousand) select neurons short. Memories of dreams unnerved me in the early morning. I went for a walk unaware of my impending death bar a subtle trembling recessed in the sub-conscience. Dr. Rotkod had mentioned the potential for faint holdover, which may explain the bizarre coincidence of the simultaneous end of my life and my last imprint on the local economy, but not that I would ever remember the conversation. I relay this now with omniscience granted by the passing of life.

I continued in such detached concentration that it was far too late by the time I realized I had missed my turn off of Vidrar Way. I came upon the wonderful smells of Bakers Lane; a unique section of the town not secluded to a single ethnicity and culture but to baking. The breads, pies, cookies here were the best in the region perhaps the world. I never traveled any farther than a bottle begged, but came here often enticed by the nostalgia of my childhood and memory of my father.

A wonderful memory is a voice, my father’s voice. A voice that always read aloud my favorite story, a clever tale about a knight:

Once upon a time there was a Knight who had to prepare for a dangerous battle with the foulest of trolls in the land of Rheinmark. He had faced many enemies, and was often victorious. He had slain dragons and kings. But the stories of the Troll's terror frightened him. The Troll placed his victims in burning pots of stew or ate them alive limb by limb. The knight decided that he could not lose his honor in any other manner other than death. He would face death for that is the code of Knights. Now, we have the pleasure of meeting that brave Knight

I placed my suit of armor on and after a hesitation left the house. A poor beggar in rags sat outside my door in the alley. He threatened all who passed near him. I approached him and invited him to my life’s end, and explained that I must fight the most powerful Knight in the land. “Your life’s end is already here!” he threatened, as he attacked me with an iron blade. I grabbed at his wrist, but fell victim to the unpredictability of the beginner. His iron blade slipped under my shoulder plate and into fragile flesh. All the worry over the battle vanished with the intense pain. Anger flashed quickly through my veins and in seconds I had the beggar by the throat following a swift thrust of his pitiful self into the stonewall. The irony, I thought, that through the iron of a beggar I almost meet my life’s end.

I remember my father took me often to a place with the most ambrosial bread. Tasting that bread ruined a man. All other loaves were insipid bricks by comparison.

La Boulangerie du Renomme was nestled in the cobbled corners of the quarter, where crowds of tourists, locals and jealous bakers jostled for the loaves of scare supply. My father had grown up with Renomme in Bakers Lane, and worked his first job with Renomme at Renomme’s bakery. He would still help Renomme on occasion so we never had to wait.

I was a precocious baker and baked my own bread with Patrice, Renomme’s sister, while my father helped with the baking and chatted with Renomme. Renomme rarely had time for chatting since many a sniveling bake would buy one of each loaf to discern its secrets (imported French seed grown on a family farm) and many tourists made La Boulangerie du Renomme their number one stop in the smallish city of Halvemaen.

Flour hung in the air like morning fog over coastal bridges, and lightened the dark features of my father. The pace was fast but not in the sense that it was rushed, rather purposeful and enveloped in the joviality of a man doing what he loved, baking, and another doing what he truly loved, living. This was a cherished memory, and one of a collection of memories of my father, which I could never replicate with my own sons.

I did not love living. I loved numbing and excluding myself from living. I filtered life out with alcohol and gave my sons a collection of scars, not memories.

As a small child, I thought I was the only person to in one instant wish horrific death on an innocent dog licking my face, then an instant later be in tears for having considered such a wretched act.I remember my father’s revelation to me of the prevalent flickers of horrific thoughts, of the intrusive thoughts as I came to learn about them in my psychological study.

We encircled Serpent Lake in Hyde Park during a final trip to London, the eve of my father’s final battle with brain cancer. My father was a vehement Psychoanalyst, but the gentlest and most calming man I know. His voice was an Arvo Pärt symphony, at all times a eulogy, full of hope and security. It was with that voice of a rabbit resting he spoke to me the day before he died.

“Throughout life you will face terrible attacks from the inner demons. They will come in many forms, and defy your internal fortifications. One form is most despicable. Impulse will pray on a natural defense. His finger alit with fire and smoke will probe into your mind and inject in an instant thoughts and desires only the extremity of evil could conceive. You must never follow these desires even if they appear endemic orders. Learn to immediately block them out of mind and shut the door on impulsive meddling.”

In an instant he was dead. The following day he never awoke. Time may have pacified the friedliche Sprache but the voice survives in memory. Only through my memory and I, he survives. I could not deny my sons similar memories. I could not deny myself immortality by continuing to test their threadbare loyalty, less cared for than my Ferghan.

4. Legend's End

I found a depressing scene at La Boulangerie du Renomme. Hoards of rats not customers covered the floor. Orders were not called out, rather a sorry oven coughed and smoked. A damp musty smell of rot, unfixed leaks, and stale flour swathed the scene. The sight of an abandoned La Boulangerie recalled reality. I had lived this place in memory and in memory it was always an exciting and pleasant experience, but now I knew those times were gone, forever gone, and that permanence deeply saddened me. This was never to be a place where I was young for I would never again be young. This was never to be a place of tourism, for that time had its turn and is past. This was a dead place. I broke down into tears. I would soon join it. The tragedy of life: a gift at times so spectacularly beautiful yanked away at a moment’s notice or slowed into a different sort of drawn out agonizing pain. Yet without unintended death, our art would suffer and all would suffer. Art finds much inspiration in death. Without death, resources would be scarce and regardless, murder would reach a plateau of accepted normality. Stepwise my logic had progressed.

At his peak, Renomme was the most famous baker in the entire world. Today he was known to bake a weeks worth of bread for a single day or would bake only pain or only rye or would sometimes not bake at all rather wander the streets in search of a cat from his childhood. He was without family, without friends, without customers, without much function of his memory. Maybe a rat will endure a maze for bread, but not a member of an instant society. Some bakers helped him, but had businesses they needed to run and could only do so much for their former idol. The remaining envious considered his fall justice. Renomme should have retired and entered a home but he was a legend and the end of a legend does not come with ease.

I rubbed salty tears from my world-worn face and vowed to see this place into a proper closure. My peripatetic travels that day had drawn me here for some purpose. So from that day on for many weeks I returned to the shop of my youth. Now, to find Renomme.

I soon learned Renomme often wandered the dense web of cobbled lanes, peaking down every alley and lifting every mat in front of every door of the many busy bakeries. Customers were not happy to oblige him their place in line for the mere satisfaction of his deranged curiosity.

“I have found Chaton!” I announced as I approached Renomme peering desperately into the window of a butcher’s shop.

“Oh where? I have searched for her all day?” questioned Renomme.

His Mediterranean tone was only slightly visible beneath an uneven coating of flour like my Father had once wore flour. His bristled black hairs, a few white, stuck out from under his white hat, while under the hat remained only a far receded hairline. His nose was narrow, long with a small bump and below a smallish mouth. Short legs that matched the short arms supported his stout frame. His clothes were disheveled and unflattering of his awkward roundness.

“Chaton was asleep in the window of your shop as I past, nestled in the comforts of a deep corbeille à pain.”

“Oh we must hurry back then. If this claim rings true, I must reward you”

I led the renowned Renomme back to his shop and on arrival the fleeting quality of his memories again appeared. Despite the memory afflictions, Renomme had retained his youthful sight. He navigated the twists and turns of neatly stacked loaves of miche with agile precision and disappeared into the kitchen, Chaton forgotten for another day.

Renomme fired up the ovens again and was in argument with himself. I took his opportunity of the chef-distracted to pull the stacks and stacks of loaves fast fungus fodder out onto the street. I arranged them into a precarious tower and placed a sign in front signaling passerby’s of a unique bread deal: loaves for mere donations. With refrigeration and careful cutting, they would withstand the weekend, which was a time to be social, and to be social is to eat.

People eat and drink with others to mutually enhance experiences that alone can be mundane. A first date is often paired with coffee or a fine wine. Humans are the sum of their metabolic processes and these processes are heightened by coffee or ignored by alcohol, but created by bread. So one did not want to run out of bread during the long weekend, when few to no shops were open. Many villagers picked up the extra loaf from Renomme for a modest donation, and afforded an old man some bread of his own.

A beautiful young woman passed me as I erected the bread tower. Her eyes were a beautiful brown and hazel, a stark similarity to the wares of an Alpen chocolatier. I knew her. Or did I? Perhaps I met her on my last visit, but there were many young women who passed by and I never remember seeing such a sumptuous woman, one whom I would have remembered. Could a woman with such glow be erased so easily? I glanced into the store, nothing but a bread maze. I hurried in but she was gone. Had I lost so much time in my thoughts? I must return. I must find her again. Not even the anticipation of the morning’s first drink gave me such excitement.

Through the winter I came everyday to see her but she never came. Everyday I found the phantom cat. Everyday I waited two long hours in the afternoon in front of La Boulangerieem until Renomme threatened to call the police or his grandson to remove me, a man his elder, from loitering. Everyday I would see her walking down the street, with a confidence exuding from her every closing strut. Everyday it was but a second’s deception, morphing a common woman into my muse. Everyday I accepted this and returned to my worrisome family.

Gabriel came often to try to pull me from my misapprehensions, “Father, you must not chase delusions of your degrading mind,” he pleaded.

“Delusions! I am robust, and in perfect mental health, do not think I do not know what I am doing”

"You stand there everyday awaiting an unknown, an unseen, an unimportant.”

“Absolutely not! I await a woman, and despite being an old man, I want to end this life with love, with the reds of romance, not the dark grays of a murky dismal loneliness"

"End? With Romance? I want to understand this. Had I seen the woman, maybe I could understand, but please just leave, come home. We miss you.”

"Eh, lies, rotten lies. I had thought to spend more time there but not in that environment. Dante and you run everywhere. You incite punishment. You move in a tornado through my house with such disturbance all the thoughts in my head are shaken out, spread out on the floor, trampled, and imbibed an early death."

"For once you are not viewing the world only through the distorted future of a bottle bottom! No, instead you chase sober delusions, but a move from the bottle, despite direction, is a positive move according to us."

"Through the bottom of the bottle? Speak not in ruse, or ill chase that with a bruise. And not these sober delusions you articulate"

"Your rhymes are childish. I cannot come here everyday and shed a tear for you. Please do come to your senses.”

Gabriel left in frustration.

“I am on the brighter side of the fences, you will see in future tenses.”

I went inside, frustrated with another fruitless day and thankless children.

"Renomme, so why have you not retired? With such success, you should be in a yacht, back in France, in Nice, sipping wine all day and enjoying the luxury of a life of hard work"

"Idleness on a bed of pillows wallowing in a swallow of water? No thanks! My work is not work in the sense you believe rather a purpose. Without this purpose I am without life"

"Baking is your purpose? There is nothing else you would rather do?"

“Yes. Everyone must have a purpose and mine lies in the art of wheat and yeast: bread. Without a purpose one work’s for nothing, and happiness impossible to maintain.”

“I see truth in your reasoning. I have since abandoned my work for a bigger purpose: to find this woman. I do not seek a mate, or maybe I do? I do not know what answers she may have or motivations she may provide me but I have an intuition that I must find her. What about your family Renomme, were you ever married?

"Enough with this conversation. I must check the miche."

Resolute in my confidence and dedication I remained. I never missed a day, two hours a day, from the first flake to freeze to the last one to melt, I waited. Two hours a day without my sons. Two hours a day extrapolated over a winter amounts to days wasted. I missed days of memories. I welcomed a premature death to loiter in front of a bakery of senility. I spent a winter, my last, heaped in anticipation for a woman I would leave in death as my wife left me. I would leave the woman with the threat of alcoholism and the care of children she had no stake in. Again I looked at my sons through a bottle darkly, but a bottle of failed consideration rather than spurn.

5. Patience Through the Seasons

The rustle of autumn is the rustle of exasperation, of an end as leaves leave. A long time apart from water they have dried and cracked in the dry cold of approaching winter. A pair of feet on an afternoon’s walk compresses the dried, brittle remains crushing the former ripe leaf, now a lifeless shadow, into folic dust.

The rustle of spring is an antithesis of the rustle of autumn, and rightly so—it’s a rustle of beginning not end. The Sunday afternoons, and a new mom for my sons, the woman with the schockolade eyes, were so innocent and beautiful in nature, yet a subconscious voice pawed at the surface. 6. Ante Arbore

The wealth of centuries, of my family’s history was scattered throughout my office. The impressive glint of a sword—light abstractions—retracted my pupils.

The animal instinct is so involuntary. My frontal lobe relinquished processing authority to the primitive reptilian hindbrain. A match fluttered alit, fell and tumbled match head over matchstick onto my latest writing. The words with the obverse ease in which they were written, vanished. I grabbed the pages in front of me, to read them, to watch the fire read them, consuming each word leaving behind ash, the words transformed. The ignited ashes detached and floated around the room onto the threads of the Ferghan, woven in the year of the first crusade. Flames fluttered, entropic movement according to the dynamics of present oxygen and the health of the source: a one-of-a-kind rug formed into the holy tatters of the carpet of a downtrodden poet too lost in his work to segregate ashes to a tray.

I became fixated on the loss of wealth before my eyes; the carpet’s reduction to a black worthless ash pile, carbon and smoke forever appropriated throughout the atmosphere, thrilled me. The loss of responsibility for an object, the anxiety over its preservation, the endless professional cleanings, and wealth put forth to protect this vault of wealth, excited me.

I stamped it out! I rescued the remains! Not out of a return of a need neither to perpetuate this anxiety nor to continue to enjoy the constant upkeep of a museum piece, but because the same destruction could occur without such senselessness. Why feed all my possessions to fire when I could donate them? I could sell them to give my sons an insurance of a decent life, and donate the rest to caring hands. They could not be given to the government, or the ministry of a demonstration of the shortcomings of human nature, as the news so often inspires me to name it. No, perhaps an orphanage or a museum of the Allemanne family would be better. All will be donated, the history of the pieces taught, and the richness of the collection maintained in knowledge, not quantified currency.

I took a step out the door, and down the stone steps. The blustery winds blew over the river and forced my body to a rigid bend, my shoulders hunched, face parallel to the ground, as the top of my head bravely faced the sting of cold. I resisted the winds push and raised my head in a reverse of the rigid duck I was forced into earlier. I paused and became entranced by the eternal slow flow of the river a couple hundred meters from my door.

The humid sea breeze lulled me into a mind-wandering vision. In a single breath past my eyes played out the history of this river, a maritime battlefield of no match in history. Before my eyes castles fell; ships sunk; marauders came and went, drowned and pillaged; explorers arrived for the greatness of intellect rather than the greatness of the sword; barges replaced warships; garish tourist ships appeared; in a fast approach a Viking and his axe crashed into my vision!

I opened my eyes and stretched up to see nothing but the historied river pushing on into a future far more enduring than mine.

This section of earth had rotated away from sun, and darkness fell. Herr Lehman stepped out the gatehouse as I approached the end of the drive. He unfastened the locks and pushed the large iron gate open. Before slipping through the gap, I approached him.

“Herr Lehman, hold out your hand if you would”

“For what reason Herr Allemanne?”

“Please, do as I say, the sooner you do, the sooner the answer is revealed”

“Yes, sir”

Into his hand I set a velvet bag weighed down by a decades salary for a gatekeeper.

“Your services are no longer needed here, Mr. Lehmann. I appreciate the unwavering loyalty and hard work. Here is your salary in full for the next ten years, a wager based on the best guess I could make for how much longer I would have lived”

“I cannot take this, Dr. Allemanne. I cannot accept a wager on your life. Excuse me Sir but I am overwhelmed at the moment. I do not understand.”

“Oh, no need to understand, appreciate the mystery. Now see that this gate is refastened and I wish you and your family the best.”

“But, Sir -”

His voice quieted behind my hurried pace towards the Cathedral.

As I crossed the city, I made a regretful decision to enter Reganplatz. I was an unarmed civilian in a swarm of enemy soldiers. From every side bulky bags and pointed corners of shoeboxes wounded me. Consumerism transformed into an army of senseless selfish robots battering me without conscience. I felt so insecure, so out of place. Not a friendly eye, yet here is happiness being bought by the bagful — happiness that will subside as quickly as the swipe of a card, unlike happiness of an experience, which is idiosyncratic thus resistant to the decay of social comparison. Like the hounds of Chateau Chaverny, a group frothed at the entrance to their next short-lived high waiting inches behind the closed store glass. Impulse abounds.

Today was also a sports day. The city brimmed with jungle cries from citizens dressed for battle, in decorative patterns of three colors that solidified in-group membership, and some believed, superseded common decency. Halvemaen played against another city, which I lack the intimate knowledge of the sport (whichever one it was this winter season) to relate. Even in this state of omniscience, my apathy persists. A menagerie of players with no connection to this city played against a menagerie from another city, equal in vague connection. The result was Halvemaen cheering for a brand, the colors and shapes that never changed in contrast to the players who always changed. In a fault of misattribution, they believed love of city meant love of the brand, and sought to confirm their own preconception of loving a city with biased interpretations and memories. They believed their brand to be the best becoming hostile when their superiority was not respected. To the threatening mascot, the fans loyalty lied, and to ridiculous violence they defended the brand. Their testosterone rose and fell with their team, and like the rise and fall of musical notes, played out a tragic scene in man.

At the end of the platz was St. Anthony’s Cathedral, whose disproportionately tall openwork spires sprawled into the heavens like the spreading branches of a tree. The cathedral rested on the border of the far reaches of my estate and its muddy trails prevented swifter passage. The aged charcoal cathedral seemed a stone skeleton with a vaulted torso, cribbed flying buttresses and clerestory windows coming to flamboyant arches. St. Anthony’s displayed the underlying order and rationality of the universe in its geometrical and mathematical construction, and a collateral aesthetic of magnificence. From the Himmelturm, or heaven tower, one could look out and see almost all directions the edge of the city as it met the edge of a present twilight sky. In only one direction, was the view hindered.

The Himmelturm was shadowed by our Yggdrasil, so named for its similarity to the tree of life of Norse mythology, a Fraxinus excelsior or Ash tree of atypical dimensions. The pollard tree bulged at the bottom, and progressively narrowed towards the top where it became a thicket of branches. The arms were bare and stretched to all directions. Here I made my home.

I knocked on the great doors of St. Anthony’s in hope the monks had finished evening prayer and could have me. I could not wait until the sun came around again.

The great door opened with the reluctance, speed and fluidity of a languorous giant. A modest monk in drab gray invited me in with cheerfulness unexpected of one in such ascetic dress.

“Dr. Allemanne, if you are here for our latest Trappist brew, we will not open the cellar for another hour. But we are pleased to see you and have missed you the past few months. Is everything alright with your family?” I crossed the threshold and stepped aside as Luke shoved closed the giant door.

“Dr. Allemanne where are you going?”

I traversed the transept, reached the end of the sanctuary, climbed up the stairs past the altar and slipped behind into a narrow passage, the semita latenter. The passage was but a few footfalls before I reached a dead end. Before me was a rod-iron ladder.

“Dr. Allemanne, no has been up that ladder since I took my vows here almost 25 years ago. It is not safe! Please Sir, come down! Tell me what it is you wanted to say. I must be hurrying back to my chambers to prepare for this evenings Bible study.”

The ladder held, and the height was dizzying, but the view spectacular. Luke sat against the curved wall to the right of me with his legs scrunched and pressed up to his chest.

“Afraid of heights? I used to be but now this height does not seem any higher to me than that first step on the ladder. Fear is only psychological and can be overcome with cunning.”

Himmelturm had a large hollowed cone for a roof and earned its name from its 150-meter height, a skyscraper of its day. When the church had been built centuries earlier, this tower was not in the original design. Sir Bakermary did not believe he was testing God, only demonstrating his faith.”

The platform was the shape of a toothless key. Afraid of heights, he rotated and then let himself fall backwards, expected to be caught by a gust of heavenly wind. His faith buckled under his weight and in horrid awe the crowd below watched him fall terminal at terminal velocity. They scattered, none brave or strong enough to break his lengthy fall. His petrified wife made no movement. All the weight fell on her, and together they were forced into the earth from whence they came, dead, in such tragedy. The church condemned Bakermary as reckless, and sought to distance itself from one who would dare test god, a god who valued mindless conformity and obedience of custom and authority over human life.

I dangled my legs above the Bakermary’s grave, and invited Luke to at least turn his head out over the grounds, and my acres. “See, Yggdrasil out there Luke”

“Yes, Sir, the ash tree”

“From the mansion at the far end to the tree on the border, and all in-between, from the far fence out past the well,” I pointed west “to the iron fence below, that marks the border between my property and Gods, I give that to the church, to your church, Luke.”

“That is a very generous donation, Sir. Abbot Wilhelm will be very pleased. You must certainly be a wellspring of God’s pride. I thank you. The church thanks you. That old ash tree will be the first thing to go. “Yggdrasil stays. I apologize for not mentioning it earlier but the tree stays. Do you have any extra robes?”

“Robes? Sir”

“Yes, a drab gray one such as yours. You must keep extras for those who may answer God’s call in the future”

“Oh, these robes,” Luke in his humble style pinched a section of the rough, dry, sackcloth forward, “Abbot Wilhelm should have a few spare in his office. We monks are not as neat as one may think, and this cloth although cheap attracts dirt far more easily than one likes”

“Sir! 5 Geld is a year’s tithe for some of our members. That is not necessary. Your generous donation will cover the modest cost of a monk’s cloth. But may I ask for what reason you are requesting a robe? Your clothes are the finest in the city.” “Here is 5 Geld, now I have wasted much time enjoying this spectacular view and must be off. Please hurry back with the robe.” I dropped the coins into the pockets of his robe.

Luke’s confounded mind slowed to a pensive pause, mouth gaping, but I ignored them, walking to the edge. Luke descended into the tower.

I pulled my decorative kurta, a gift from an Indian prince, over my head, and tossed the kurta up into the air like a bird first learning to fly. In the wind it became an unmasted sail not resisting the push and pull of the wind. The garment settled on the grass far below, soon joined by the rest of my pretentious clothes. I stood at the edge of the diving platform, imagining a loch of the Scottish highlands inviting me from below. The wind gusts were strong and near constant. A thought of Lucifer’s, one entered prying and prodding in my mind, to test God, to lean forward just a little and the let God’s Grace assist in my defiance of gravity, but could not, for I did not harbor the faith of Bakermary. No I had an instinctual drive for preservation that lay incognito, secluded from the meddling of the dangerous consciousness.

“Sir, Dr. Allemanne, please step back, my heart is pounding from here. I have brought two robes, one gray like mine and another purple like the Abbots, which I think may be a better wear for a man of your stature. I also insist that your previous donations are satisfactory. Please keep the 5 Geld, and insure a philanthropic life for your sons. I insist sir.”

“No the gray one will do, and my sons have enough. 5 Geld will insure them nothing but material of this world. I ask that you take them in, care for them, and teach them of faith, and belief, which is the only currency that can buy what is truly important. I must go now. They will be finished with their studies, soon. Ill have Ms. Steinhurst bring them by.”

I had provided Ms. Steinhurst with a salary, but for her projected life not mine. She became the legal guardian of my sons from now until her death.

From the cathedral, I turned not right to home, but left to follow along the road that brought the wheat and fruits of the surrounding farmers into town. I once had not even considered a foot upon this peasant path. Anytime I had to cross, I required my driver cover it in a rich red velvet carpet for my feet, which I believed deserved better than even the most clever peasant mind.

I wandered along the path for quite some time as the houses became less densely packed and gave way to large pastures with cows, goats and sheep grazing. Dusk came and went. Moonshine lit the path behind me. I had never walked so long and so far, not since I was a boy.

Exerted, I reached and rang the knocker of the Bauer household. Nothing.

I knocked again.

An inquisitive light came on with murmurs, then a loud, “Who comes by a tired farmers door so late in the night during the harvest?”

“Dr. Allemanne. I could use a place to rest my—”

“We don’t have your money! You took it all last month. We must live on the small garden in the back now.”

“Please, I do not come to collect debts. Let me in, and let me rest a bit. I have business to discuss.”

Silence until sunrise. I huddled next to the door in chilled reluctant sleep.

Before the sun, another light awoke me. The door creaked open behind me. A work-worn face, with permanent bags hanging beneath the eyes and a few gray hairs poking out around the chin greeted me in twilight of an important day. Herr Bauer, a man in his early looking 60 but actually in his 40s invited me in.

I entered the modest house. Inside, there was a wooden table, nicked and dented with charming peeled paint. Next to the table was a medieval fireplace below many hanging black pots. The fireplace smoldered mild warmth in subtle reflection on the soot-stained walls. Simple and cozy. “ Please, sit down–Dr. Allemanne, you said?” Herr Bauer said.

“Yes, that is I.”

“But you are dressed as Monk. Have you been drinking all night and into the morning at the Trappist’s and ruin your clothes? Religious men they say, but never seem to deny you drink or your generous offers of alms that follow.”

His wife approached the table. “Would you like a drink, all I have to offer is peasants brew?”

“I find that quite apt for these robes, a peasant brew sounds delicious.”

She brought us each a cup. I took a long sip and felt the caffeine taking command of my neural chemistry. The caffeine binds to the neurons and blocks adenosine’s slowing effects. The block signals emergency and adrenaline and dopamine are released, a natural energized high.

“No I have adopted a more spartan lifestyle, one that does not require such lavish raiment. I have passed my fortune onto the Abbey for their school and am here to cancel your debts and assure your children a proper education.”

He rubbed his puzzled old face.

“Now, was it about 2 years ago that I financed your new barn?”

“I believe, so. We had Martina heading off to private school and could not afford both her education and a new barn. Then our prices at the market were forced down by competition from other towns, but interest on our debts increased. We had to borrow or fail.”

“Friend, and truly I mean friend, I regret that past of mine. I come here today to cancel that debt.”

“Sir I cannot settle any debts. I can barely afford my payments. Please leave, leave if that is what you are here for. Unless you want this shirt from my back. It’s soaked with more work than you have ever imagined behind your antique desk, golden pens, and wealth built upon the precarious backs of us workers!”

I let him vent. I let him express. Then intervened, not wanting him to be too worked up. Too stressed. I know how stress ages one.

“Perhaps you misheard. I said cancel not settle these debts and will accept nothing but agreement in this. As of today, all the geld you owe me is null and void. Here is all the interest that have you paid in full. I have no need for it. Thank you for the coffee. I must go and make many more visits.”

Herr Bauer transformed from an expression of desperate stress to disbelieving elation. I had no time and left him.

And on and on I went, house to house throughout the countryside. I visited all my debtors and canceled all the debts to astonishment, disbelief and at the end of weapons. My pockets lightened, as did my heart. A day later I entered town elated, almost ready to retreat to my shell. One more important visit remained.

I entered the sterile foyer and felt as aesthetically pleased as I would in a hospital. The furniture was perfect but a style fitting of hotel lobbies and dim waiting rooms, impersonal and exuding corporate banality. Furniture for one who created the safe and overdone, with class of decades before, if one would even say those post-war decades had class. Not even dust seemed to have used the cloy flowers and dull patterns to rest. I chose to stand.

The Mayor’s office, like the foyer, was elite but impractical, exorbitant but uncomfortable.

“Mikhail, I am here on behalf of our farmers. They cannot afford the drive into Halvemaen. Many plan to cut themselves off entirely and only grow what they need, return to the simple life. “Have you ever tasted those canned vegetables? They are only enticing in their manipulative decoration.”

“Unlike you, I am not motivated by altruism. I don’t succumb to the “good feelings” of “good deeds.” Disparity is the function of nature. “This is not paranoia. Her talent, her word, her story will become viral, will spread and infect the mind and push out the strict patriotic thought that permeates the misinformed. Illusions of safety have superseded liberty, fraternity is as empty as the regard two passing strangers hold for each other, and inequality emerges in even the most trivial ways.”

“While this all sounds unfortunate, you cannot expect me to shoot myself in the foot.”

“We both know you would suffer at most a prick. You have enough savings to live ten times as comfortable as the farmers. Instead you live upon them and the support is faltering. I have retired to a monk’s life and will no longer be an accomplice to this profligate existence.”

7. Yggdrasil

“Nichts ist heiliger, nichts ist vorbildlicher als ein schöner, starker Baum. Ein Baum spricht: In mir ist ein Kern, ein Funke, ein Gedanke verborge, ich bin Leben vom ewigen Leben. Einmalig ist der Versuch und Wurf, den die ewige Mutter mit mir gewagt hat, einmalig ist meine Gestalt und das Geäder meiner Haut, einmalig das kleinste Blätterspiel meines Wipfels und die kleinste Narbe meiner Rinde. Mein Amt ist es, im ausgeprägten Einmaligen das Ewige zu gestalten und zu zeigen.”

[“Nothing is sacred, nothing is more exemplary than a beautiful, strong tree. A tree says: Hidden within me is a core, a seed, a thought. I am life from eternal life. As the conversation drew to a natural close, he rapped twice feigning a fidgety demeanor. A warning, Gabriel thought. Gabriel stowed himself behind some luggage. His finger throbbed in disproportional pain.

“201 Geld”

“211 Geld, new bidder!”

“250 Geld”

“300 Geld”

“375 Geld”

Dante held out.

"A warning, 375 million," announced the auctioneer.

The bidding was methodical among the anxious palpable crowd.

Every new bid followed by collective exasperation.

“300 Geld, 400, 425, 450, 475, 490, 525!” The impatience pushed the bidding up, faster.

“550 Geld!”

“600 Geld!”

“650!”

“750”

In seconds, the cost doubled, then tripled, burgeoning to a bid of 1500 Geld.

The bid surprised everyone, surprised Dante though it came from his mouth.

“This is to be a record, my fare men and women. Fair warning, I am selling it this time. Going once, going twice,-”

A commotion erupted at the back of the hall. An old smuggler with a torch barrelled through as fast as the crowd could part.

“Its a fake. I’m sorry I sold the real one to a man from Anderseite. I’m a terrible traitor and come now to admit my grave mistake, please do, as you must. Oh, I am such a despicable traitor!” Drummond thrust himself forward, opened his grip and hurled the torch at the painting.

Drummond was struck down with a successive shots from the crowd.

Smugglers returned shots at his attacker, a distinguished man brandishing an antique pistol, far too slow to reload.

All manner of defensive arms emerged as the scene collapsed into chaos of fleeing, death, blood, and visceral action.

Dante abandoned the suffocating robe and ran around two men exchanging death by sword.

Gabriel had been on the road for two weeks stowed in the back the coach, and unable to carry out his plan of stealing the chariot. His hand throbbed beneath the makeshift wrapping on his hand. A disgusting odor and sign of blackened gangrene skin infested Gabriel’s nose. He gagged at the sight. His cut was infected. The gangrene ebbed down his arm, from the knick on his finger and invaded his blood. His body succumbed under the stress of a fever, weakened further by the weeks of a poor diet. Gabriel lay in a damp pool of sweat subject to an early death and evident of his existence borne into a trap. Alessandria wondered eternal.

Dr. Samekh, now after his vows known as Brother Vehere, kneeled down for his evening prayer. Throughout the day, at each prayer he noticed a gathering in the yard below around Dr. Allemanne, the tree. The crowd grew with the forward arch of the sun and beyond the suns disappearance. Now the tree was alight in the gleam of a hundred angered torches.

Rumors and gossip were sins, and conversation consisted rarely of anything but interpretations of the Bible, so all the news Dr. Allemanne heard came from what he overheard patrons discussing after the end of the service. That evening before his final prayer, he heard of a gathering at Yggdrasil by Peir and his followers.

Gathered around the tree were insular minds of the average populace, contributing nothing to culture and on a pathway of returning to the earth having made no change upon the world. An intellectually inferior man of action, Peir Gerlofs led the group. Considering his obvious lack of appreciation for education, he strived to make cogent arguments about the path of society, but always equated this path with that which birthed a genocidal maniac and his both active and passive followers, all of whom were accessories to a murder of millions. Peir Gerlofs and his followers, like those of the manic, stared with the dead eyes of an instinctual animal, unable to actively process the true meaning of their actions.

Peir Gerlofs had long preached claims of vivid images of death and suffering that came from the supposed black magic in the tree. Subsequently the media reprinted these images encouraging them to be repeated frequently in conversation, heightening the emotional arousal in the sensitive average; they became victims of their own subconscious acting irrationally and protective. Though the probability and likelihood of black magic very low, the ruminations of potential discomfort were too difficult to ignore, thus logic was ignored.

Martina had founded this group but could not cure their emergent toxic ideology. They were a volatile mass driven by pluralistic ignorance, resistant to all argument and truthful revelation. Martina tried to point out the flaws of their reasoning, but was ostracized. No single member willing to admit that they do not believe what they believed all believed.

Match strikes, torches thrown, and in seconds am I bleeding energy with the efficiency of a healthy flame. The bright orange and reds are reflected in the heavens.

The timid crowd retreats surprised by the fierce wave of splendid energy warming their cold faces.

Yggdrasil transforms into a beacon of light, radiating red-yellow into all corners of town.

Red-yellows color the sky.

Burnt wood fills chill air; smoke rises into the cosmos; cinders fall.

Alessandria waits at In Vino Veritas for Gabriel, whom time will never bring.

Dr. Samekh kneels, eyes-closed, with slow steady breaths at final prayer.

The longest, thickest branch, Yggdrasil’s mast, swings disconnected from the trunk and obliterates the tower.

Dr. Samekh and the church collapse onto Peir’s, onto his torched followers.

An ethereal song fades in. The notes tell the tale of life. The music expresses joy itself, sadness itself, elation itself—emotions stripped from their contextual stories.

“A sky deep azure, a navy of shifting waters mirror with iridescence like cascading ocean waves. So vast. A silhouette forever on the night sky, behold me!”
