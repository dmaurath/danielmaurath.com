Smoke of a dense central furnace disintegrating into a diaphanous periphery flowed up in a slow sway of frigid final breath.

With the fall of a hammer I was free. Free of the constant burden of respiration. Free of the irrational fear of death. Free of my last possession, the final artifact of my consumerism: Hieronymus Bosch’s The Temptation of St. Anthony—an exquisite priceless piece that is a surreal evocation of the ever inspirational theme of St. Anthony’s temptations in the desert. Anthony resisted temptress phantoms and Demons manifested as spurious offers of wealth and violent tormenters that infiltrated his every waking moment. Now he is appealed for deliverance from St Anthony’s Fire, an infectious disease of the skin named in his direct honor.

St. Anthony's Fire is caused by ergot fungus in spoiled rye. Symptoms include: seizures, spasms diarrhea, parenthesis, itching, headaches, nausea, vomiting, and gangrene that emerges at the fingertips and toe tips then spreads until at once the dead, black tissue meets as one, united.

Athanasius of Alexandria put all the stories of St Anthony to paper in an influential biography Vita S Antoni. The spread of St Anthony’s life’s stories and the ideas they withheld led to the rise of monasticism across the west. This is the story of all that precedes the freeing hammer fall. Like consciousness, this story is a confluence of thoughts and ideas, of both natural and human artistic expressions, concise but dense with mind-wandering nutrients, which I can only hope precipitates a fraction of Vita S Antoni’s influence.
